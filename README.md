<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>A

<p class=MsoNormal><b><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Mathematical
Foundation<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><b><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Probability
Space<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>All
stochastic models are grounded in a formal probability space $(\Omega, \<span
class=SpellE>mathcal</span>{F}, \<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{P})$, where
$\Omega$ is the sample space of all possible market outcomes, $\<span
class=SpellE>mathcal</span>{F}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra of measurable events, and
$\<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{P}$ is a probability measure satisfying
Kolmogorov's axioms. Options pricing introduces a second measure $\<span
class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{Q}$ &#8212; the <b>risk-neutral measure</b> &#8212;
under which all assets drift at the risk-free rate $r$. The Black-Scholes price
is always computed as an expectation under $\<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{Q}$,
not $\<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{P}$.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><b><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Geometric
Brownian Motion<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Stock
prices are modelled by the stochastic differential equation (SDE):<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$$\frac{<span
class=SpellE>dS_t</span>}{<span class=SpellE>S_t</span>} = \mu , dt + \sigma , <span
class=SpellE>dW_t</span>$$<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>where:<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<ul style='margin-top:0cm' type=disc>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l2 level1 lfo2;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$<span class=SpellE>S_t</span>$
     = stock price at time $t$<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l2 level1 lfo2;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$\mu$ = drift
     (real-world expected return, annualised)<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l2 level1 lfo2;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$\sigma$ = volatility
     (std dev of log-returns, annualised)<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l2 level1 lfo2;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$<span class=SpellE>dW_t</span>$
     = increment of a standard Brownian motion<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Applying
<span class=SpellE>It&ocirc;'s</span> Lemma &#8212; which extends classical
calculus to stochastic processes via the correction term
$\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} dt$ arising from $(<span
class=SpellE>dW_t</span>)^2 = dt$ &#8212; yields the closed-form solution:<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$$S_T
= S_0 \exp!\left[\left(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)T + \sigma W_T\right]$$<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>The
$-\<span class=SpellE>tfrac</span>{1}{2}\sigma^2$ <span class=SpellE>It&ocirc;</span>
correction is essential: without it, Jensen's inequality applied to the convex
exponential would produce an upward-biased expectation. The correction exactly
restores $\<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{E}[S_T] = S_0 e^{\mu T}$.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><b><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Black-Scholes
Analytical Pricing<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Under
the risk-neutral measure $\<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{Q}$, the drift
$\mu$ is replaced by $r$, and the price of a European call option is:<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$$C
= S_0 , \Phi(d_1) - K e^{-<span class=SpellE>rT</span>} \Phi(d_2)$$<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$$P
= K e^{-<span class=SpellE>rT</span>} \Phi(-d_2) - S_0 , \Phi(-d_1)$$<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>where
$\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF and:<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$$d_1
= \frac{\ln(S_0 / K) + \left(r + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}},
\<span class=SpellE>qquad</span> d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>with:<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<ul style='margin-top:0cm' type=disc>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$S_0$ = current stock
     price<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$K$ = strike price<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$T$ = time to expiry
     (years)<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$r$ = continuously
     compounded risk-free rate<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l0 level1 lfo3;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$\sigma$ = volatility<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>The
quantities $\Phi(d_2)$ and $\Phi(d_1)$ have precise probabilistic meaning:
$\Phi(d_2) = \<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{Q}(S_T &gt; K)$ is the
risk-neutral probability of exercise, and $\Phi(d_1)$ is the option's Delta
&#8212; the rate of change of price with respect to the stock price. Since $d_1
- d_2 = \sigma\sqrt{T} &gt; 0$, the Delta always exceeds the exercise probability,
reflecting that conditional on exercise the holder receives the stock, not
merely a fixed dollar.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Put-call
parity provides an exact consistency check that must hold to machine precision:<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$$C

<p class=MsoNormal><b><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Monte
Carlo Pricing<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>The
risk-neutral pricing formula $V_0 = e^{-<span class=SpellE>rT</span>} \<span
class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{E}^{\<span class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{Q}}[\text{payoff}(S_T)]$
is estimated by simulation. Drawing $N$ independent paths under $\<span
class=SpellE>mathbb</span>{Q}$ (drift set to $r$):<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>$$\hat{V}<i>0
= e^{-<span class=SpellE>rT</span>} \<span class=SpellE>cdot</span> \frac{1}{N}
\sum</i>{<span class=SpellE>i</span>=1}^{N} \text{payoff}(S_T^{(<span
class=SpellE>i</span>)})$$<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>By
the Central Limit Theorem, the standard error of this estimator scales as
$O(N^{-1/2})$: halving the error requires four times as many paths. This
convergence rate is verified empirically via log-log plots of standard error
against $N$, where the slope of $-0.5$ confirms correct implementation.<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><b><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Variance
Reduction<o:p></o:p></span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Two
techniques reduce Monte Carlo standard error for a fixed computational budget:<o:p></o:p></span></p>

<ul style='margin-top:0cm' type=disc>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l4 level1 lfo4;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><b><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Antithetic variates:</span></b><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'> For each standard
     normal draw $Z$, also evaluate the payoff at $-Z$. The negative
     correlation between the two estimates reduces variance, often by 50% or
     more for smooth payoffs.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
 <li class=MsoNormal style='mso-list:l4 level1 lfo4;tab-stops:list 36.0pt'><b><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'>Control variates:</span></b><span
     style='mso-fareast-font-family:"Times New Roman"'> Exploit the known
     analytical price of a related instrument (e.g. a geometric Asian option)
     to correct the estimator for the target (e.g. arithmetic Asian option),
     substantially reducing variance without additional simulation.<o:p></o:p></span></li>
</ul>
